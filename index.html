RESEARCH BACKGROUND

Ubiquity and limitations of paper

Although the advent of mobile computing is often discussed in tandem with the realization of a paperless office, paper remains ubiquitous in our lives, particularly as a medium for carrying information. Standardized examinations are still administered using paper forms, doctors persist on issuing patients physical notes, and students continue to read from printed articles despite the availability of digital versions. A significant amount of research has been conducted to explain the persistence of paper as information carriers in spite of recent technological advancements. Several prior studies focused on distilling out specific physical affordances of paper, such as its organizability in space and its allowance for the interweaving of the reading and writing processes. Others explored the capacity of paper in supporting collaborative activities.

Using AR to improve the efficacy of paper

Several prior studies sought to address the limitations of paper while preserving its desirable physical attributes through the means of digital augmentation. Rather than replace paper in its entirety with a digital medium, past works recognized the complementary profiles of strengths and weaknesses of paper and electronic media, and instead tried to bridge the paper-digital divide. For instance, Yeh et al. extended the capabilities of notebooks to support heterogenous information association, rich information access, and enhanced data transformation and integration. Morrison et al, studied the mobile augmentation of physical paper maps. Subramonyam, Druker, and Adar recently developed a mobile-based augmented reality application of data-assisted affinity diagramming.

Research Gap and RQs

Despite the populizarization of synergistic paper-digital systems, with an increasing number of prototypes being proposed at human-computer interaction conferences like CHI, there currently lacks a comprehensive investigation regarding

  1. 1 the dimensions that may affect the effectiveness and usefulness of paper as an information medium,
  2. 2 the strengths and weaknesses of different paper products along these dimensions, and
  3. 3 opportunities for further research and design in the area of digitally augmetned paper technologies given the dimensions identified.


Taxonomy of Attributes Framework

A Taxonomy methodology of Attributes' framework

20 attributes in total

In this paper, we adapt the economic construct of utility proposed by Toomim et al. to quantify the efficacy of paper products as information carriers. Through three phases of research activities, we develop a conceptual framework of paper utility via a systematic literature review, obtain quantitative utility measures of each dimension in the framework through a series of crowd-sourcing studies, and present the research and design opportunities identified using the proposed framework.
